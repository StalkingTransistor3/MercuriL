The problem

Navigation apps like Google Maps, Waze and Apple Maps rarely show timely, verified flood-related road closures — especially in rural Australia. Under their terms of service, these platforms disclaim duty of care for third-party data integrity, which limits what they can safely ingest.

Road authorities and emergency services do have that duty. But their closure data is fragmented across states, agencies and platforms, arrives late, and rarely reaches the driver in time. Predictive flood models exist — they infer conditions from rainfall, river gauges and terrain — but they can go offline during the exact events that matter, and they cannot reliably tell you when a road is safe to reopen.

The result is the pattern Australians know too well: cars entering water at unmonitored rural crossings, avoidable strandings, and roads left closed longer than they need to be.

The solution

Totem pairs low-cost pole-mounted sensors at flood-prone crossings with a verified-closure feed that unifies official sources and ground-truth data.

Totem — the hardware

Ruggedised, pole-mounted sensor modules deployed at flood-prone rural crossings. Each Totem measures water depth and velocity against UNSW Water Research Lab trafficability criteria — the same thresholds used by councils and emergency services to decide whether a vehicle can safely cross.

  • Target unit cost: AUD $50 – $150 per Totem
  • NB-IoT + geostationary satellite comms — works with no mobile coverage
  • Estimated satellite data cost: under AUD $5 / month / device
  • Designed for multi-decade service in the field

The verified-closure feed

A middleware pipeline that aggregates official sources — Bureau of Meteorology, state Departments of Transport, State Emergency Services — and overlays Totem ground-truth into a single, verified closure feed.

  • One canonical feed per state, harmonised nationally
  • Available to navigation platforms under an integrity-warranted API
  • Emergency-ops view for council + SES operators
  • Ground-truth reopening signal — not just closure inference

Why now

$38B → $94B
Projected annual cost of natural disasters in Australia, rising from ~$38B today to $73B by 2060 (lower-emissions) or $94B (higher-emissions).
$200M / year
Federal Disaster Ready Fund allocation for resilience projects — scaled from $50M to $200M annually.
Under $50k
Threshold under which NSW councils can procure pilots via RFQ without a formal tender process — the pilot corridor for Totem.

Low-power satellite comms, cheap sensing, and a step-change in disaster-resilience funding have all landed in the same window. The technical stack that makes a $50-per-node flood network viable is a 2026 thing, not a 2020 thing.
